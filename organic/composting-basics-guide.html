Composting Basics

Learn the fundamentals of creating nutrient-rich compost for your farm

Last Updated: March 14, 2024 | 15 min read

What is Composting?

Composting is the natural process of breaking down organic materials into a rich, dark soil amendment known as compost. This process recycles various organic materials that would otherwise be considered waste.

Benefits of Composting

Composting improves soil structure, adds nutrients, enhances water retention, and promotes beneficial microorganisms, leading to healthier plants and better yields.

Compost Materials

Green Materials (Nitrogen-Rich)

  • Grass clippings
  • Fresh plant trimmings
  • Kitchen scraps
  • Coffee grounds
  • Animal manure

Brown Materials (Carbon-Rich)

  • Dry leaves
  • Straw or hay
  • Wood chips
  • Paper products
  • Cardboard

Materials to Avoid

  • Meat and dairy
  • Oils and fats
  • Diseased plants
  • Chemically treated materials
  • Non-biodegradables

Building Your Compost Pile

The Perfect Ratio

Aim for a ratio of 3:1 brown to green materials for optimal decomposition. This provides the right balance of carbon to nitrogen.

Layer Structure

Create alternating layers for effective decomposition:

  • Bottom: Coarse browns for drainage
  • Middle: Alternating greens and browns
  • Top: Brown layer to reduce odors
  • Add water between layers

Size and Location

  • Minimum size: 3x3x3 feet
  • Partial sun to shade
  • Good drainage
  • Easy access to water

Maintaining Your Compost

Moisture Management

  • Keep as moist as a wrung-out sponge
  • Water during dry periods
  • Cover during heavy rain
  • Check moisture weekly

Aeration

  • Turn pile every 1-2 weeks
  • Mix materials thoroughly
  • Ensure oxygen circulation
  • Monitor temperature

Troubleshooting

Common Problems

  • Bad odors: Add more browns
  • Slow decomposition: Add greens
  • Too wet: Add dry materials
  • Too dry: Add water
  • Pests: Cover food scraps

Using Your Compost

When is it Ready?

Finished compost should be dark, crumbly, and smell earthy. This typically takes 2-6 months depending on conditions and maintenance.
